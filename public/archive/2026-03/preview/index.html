2,800m — Clear

Kedarkantha Trek — Day 1

We started from Sankri village at 2,800 meters. The morning was crisp and clear, the kind of Himalayan morning where you can see every ridge for fifty kilometers. The trail began through a pine forest, soft needles underfoot, shafts of light cutting through the canopy.


By noon we had crossed the first meadow — Juda Ka Talab, a frozen lake at 3,100 meters. The water was perfectly still, mirroring the peaks above. We stopped for chai and aloo parathas, the kind of meal that only tastes this good when you have earned it with your legs.


The afternoon brought the first clouds. Not threatening, but present — wisps curling around the ridgeline like the mountain was breathing. The trail steepened through rhododendron forest, the trees shorter now, bent by years of wind and snow.


At 3,400 meters the forest opened up. Alpine meadows stretched ahead, brown and gold in the winter light. The campsite at Kedarkantha Base appeared — a flat clearing with the peak looming directly above, its summit ridge sharp against the darkening sky.


As evening fell, the temperature dropped fast. We huddled in the dining tent, passing around cups of tomato soup. Someone pointed out the first stars appearing through a gap in the clouds. The Milky Way would be spectacular tonight.


Day 2 — Summit Push

The alarm rang at 3am. We dressed in every layer we had and stepped out into a world transformed. Six inches of fresh snow had fallen overnight. The sky was impossibly clear — every constellation blazing, the Milky Way a river of light from horizon to horizon.


The summit trail was steep, the snow knee-deep in places. We kicked steps, each footfall a small commitment to the mountain. At 3,600 meters, the first light touched the peaks to the east — a thin line of gold that spread like fire across the Himalayan wall.


The final ridge was exposed. Wind hit us sideways, carrying fine ice crystals that stung any exposed skin. We leaned into it, one foot after another, the summit cairn visible just ahead through the blowing snow.


At 3,800 meters, we stood on top. The Swargarohini range spread before us, peak after peak catching the morning light. Bandarpunch, Black Peak, the distant shimmer of Gangotri. For a moment, the wind dropped, and there was perfect silence.


We built a small cairn of our own, five stones balanced carefully. Someone tied a prayer flag. It snapped in the returning wind, its colors impossibly bright against the white summit — blue, white, red, green, yellow — carrying whatever wishes we had left up there into the thin, cold air.
